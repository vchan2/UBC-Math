Radicals (Roots) and Fractional Exponents


The natural definition for exponentiation as an abbreviation for multiplication holds for positive integers only. We've already chosen definitions for negative and zero exponents (as shown on the integer exponent page) which are consistent with our rules for exponents. The next step is dealing with fractional exponents.

Rational (fractional) Exponents


Fractional exponents are defined as roots, which makes sense from the rule for powers of powers:
$$(a^{1/2})^2=a^{2/2}=a^1=a,\ so\; (a^{1/2})^2=a$$
You may recall that the square root of \(a\) is the number such that when you multiply by itself, the result is \(a\). Then what we have just shown is that \(a^{1/2}\) is the square root of \(a\); in other words, \(a^{1/2}\) is another way to write \(\sqrt{a}\). You may notice that for any positive number, there are two square roots - the positive one and the negative one. For example, \(2^2 = 4\) but also \((-2)^2 = 4\). As a matter of notation, we chose \(a^{1/2} = \sqrt{a}\) to represent the positive root only. For negative numbers, the square root is not even defined! (Think about why this is the case, what happens when you square a number? Can it be negative?) In a similar way, the \(n\)-th root of \(a\) is the number such that when you multiply by itself \(n\) times, the result is \(a\). We denote this by \(\sqrt[n]{a}\). Since exponentiation with integer powers is precisely repeated multiplication, \(\sqrt[n]{a}\) is the number which when raised to the exponent \(n\) gives \(a\), which is \(a^{1/n}\). As in the case \(n = 2\), whenever \(n\) is even this is undefined for negative \(a\), but if \(n\) is odd, there is no problem. For example, \(\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2\).

Definition of Roots:

For positive numbers \(a\)
\(a^{1/2} = \sqrt{a}\) is the positive number whose square is \(a\): e.g., \(49^{1/2} = \sqrt{49} = 7\)
\(a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}\) is the positive number whose \(n\)th power is \(a\): e.g., \(125^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{125} = 5\)
For negative numbers \(a\)
\(a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}\) is the negative number whose \(n\)th power is \(a\) if \(n\) is odd: e.g., \((-8)^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{-8} = -2\)
\(a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}\) is not a real number if \(a\) is negative and \(n\) is even: e.g., \((-4)^{1/2} = \sqrt{-4} =\ ?\)
Recall the exponentiation rule \((a^n)^m = a^{nm}\). Extending this to fractions and using the above interpretation, Because fractional exponents are defined as roots, we have the definitions below. The rules for fractional exponents are the same as those for integer exponents. Thus we have:

Definitions:

\(a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}\) (\(a\) must be non-negative if \(n\) is even)
\(a^{m/n}=\sqrt[n]{a^m}=(\sqrt[n]{a})^m\) (\(a^m\) must be non-negative if \(n\) is even)

Rules for Fractional Exponents

The rules for integer exponents for multiplying, dividing and raising exponentials to powers all apply for fractional exponents. In particular, we have

\((ab)^{1/n}=a^{1/n}b^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a}\sqrt[n]{b}=\sqrt[n]{ab}\) (the root of a product is the product of the roots)
\((\frac{a}{b})^{1/n}=\frac{a^{1/n}}{b^{1/n}}=\frac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}}=\sqrt[n]{\frac{a}{b}}\) (the root of a quotient if the quotient of the roots)
