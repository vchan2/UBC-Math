Integer Exponents


Repeated addition leads to multiplication. For example,

\(\underbrace{2+2+2+2+2}_{5 \text{factors}} = 2 \cdot 5\)
Similarly, repeated multiplication leads to exponentiation. For example,

\(\underbrace{2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2}_{5 factors} = 2^5\)
Here, 5 is the exponent (sometimes called power) and 2 is the base. Notice that \(2^5\) is not the same as \(5^2\) because the first is 32 while the second is 25. We make this the definition for positive integer exponents:

Definition:

\(a^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdot \cdots \cdot a}_{n \text{factors}}\) (for \(n\) a positive integer)
Recall some properties of multiplication: it is associative, meaning \((2 \cdot 2)(2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2) = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2\), so in general, our definition above gives us

Rule:

\(a^na^m = a^{n+m}\) (for \(n,m\) positive integers)
This seems like a useful rule, so we may wish to extend it beyond positive integers. If \(m = 0\), then we have \(a^na^0 = a^{n+0} = a^n\). Then if \(a\) is not 0, we can divide both sides by \(a^n\) to get \(a^0 = 1\) for \(a \neq 0\).
What if \(m\) is a negative integer? Start with \(m = -n\). We have \(a^na^{-n} = a^0 = 1\) from above, if \(a\) is not 0. Then \(a^{-n} = 1/a^n\) if \(a\) is not 0.

The case of \(a = 0\) is interesting. On one hand, \(0^n = 0\) for any positive integer \(n\) by following the initial definition. On the other hand, \(a^0 = 1\) for any non-zero \(a\). This makes defining \(0^0\) ambiguous, and so we say that this is undefined - think of it like dividing by \(0\), it's simply not allowed in math!

If we consider \(m = n\), we get the special case \(a^na^n = a^{2n}\). More generally, we see that

Rule:

\((a^n)^m = a^{nm}\) (for \(n,m\) integers, positive if \(a = 0\))

Be careful using the following:

\(ab^n = a(b^n)\) (Notice that \(ab^n \not= (ab)^n\))
\(-b^n = -(b^n)\) (Notice that \(-b^n \not= (-b)^n\))
\(-ab^n = (-a)(b^n)\)

and watch out for this common mistake

\((a+b)^n \not= a^n + b^n\) (The power of a sum is not equal to the sum of the powers)
Another property of multiplication is that it is commutative, meaning \((2 \cdot 5)(2 \cdot 5) = (2 \cdot 2)(5 \cdot 5)\), so in general, our definition above gives us

Rule:

\((ab)^n = a^nb^n\) (for \(n\) a positive integer)
This is another useful rule; as before we can extend it beyond positive integers in a similar fashion.
The case of \(a = 0\) is interesting. On one hand, \(0^n = 0\) for any positive integer \(n\) by following the initial definition. On the other hand, \(a^0 = 1\) for any non-zero \(a\). This makes defining \(0^0\) ambiguous, and so we say that this is undefined - think of it like dividing by \(0\), it's simply not allowed in math!
Here is a summary of the definitions and rules learned here:

Definitions:

\(a^0 = 1\) (if \(a\) isn't zero)
\(a^1 = a\)
\(a^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdot \cdots \cdot a}_{n \text{factors}}\) (for \(n\) a positive integer)
\(a^{-1} = \frac{1}{a}\)
\(a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}\)

Rules for Exponents:

\(a^na^m = a^{n+m}\) multiplying powers
\(\frac{a^n}{a^m} = a{n-m}\) diving powers
\((a^n)^m = a^{nm}\) raising a power to a power
\((ab)^n = a^nb^n\) power of a product
\((\frac{a}{b})^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}\) power of a quotient
