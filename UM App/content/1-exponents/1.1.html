Integer Exponents


Repeated addition leads to multiplication. For example,

\{2^5\}

Similarly, repeated multiplication leads to exponentiation. For example,

Here, 5 is the exponent and 2 is the base. Notice that \(2^5\) is not the same as \(5^2\) because the first is 32 while the second is 25. Using this understanding of exponentiation, we have the following definitions and rules:

Definitions:

\(a^0 = 1\) (if \(a\) isn't zero)
\(a^1 = a\)
\(a^n = a*a...a\) (for \(n\) a positive integer)
\(a^{-1} = \frac{1}{a}\)
\(a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}\)

Rules for Exponents:

\(a^m*a^n = a^{m+n}\) multiplying powers
\(\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a{m-n}\) diving powers
\((a^m)^n = a^{mn}\) raising a power to a power
\((ab)^n = a^nb^n\) power of a product
\((\frac{a}{b})^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}\) power of a quotient

Note that while these definitions and rules are largely intuitive, there are a couple of places where this may not be the case:

Be careful using the following

\(ab^n = a(b)^n\) (Notice that \(ab^n \not= (ab)^n\))
\(-b^n = -(b^n)\) (Notice that \(-b^n \not= (-b)^n\))
\(-ab^n = (-a)(b)^n\)

and watch out for this common mistake

\((a+b)^n \not= a^n + b^n\) (The power of a sum is not equal to the sum of the powers)
