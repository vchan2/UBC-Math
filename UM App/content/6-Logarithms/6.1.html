Logarithms

The logarithm (base \(b\)) of a number \(x\) is the exponent to which the base must be raised in order to produce \(x\), which we denote \(log_b(x)\). Then log base \(b\) is the "undoing" exponentiation base \(b\):

if \(y=log_b(x)\) then \(b^y = x\)

Note:

For reasons that will become clear after talking about functions and exponents/logarithms as functions, we only consider logarithms with positive base \(b\).

All logarithms have some useful properties that can be derived from the properties of exponents:

Properties of Logarithms:

(M and N are positive.)

\(log_b(MN)=log_b(M)+log_b(N)\) Logarithm of a Product
\(log_b(\frac{M}{N})=log_b(M)-log_b(N)\) Logarithm of a Quotient
\(log_b(M^p)=p\ log_b(M)\) Logarithm of a Power
\(log_b(1)=0\) Logarithm of +1
\(log_b(b)=1\) Logarithm of the base

Be careful not to make the mistake of trying to use these properties when they do not apply. Neither of the following forms can be rewritten using the properties above.

\(log_b(M+N)\) No rules for the logarithm of a sum
\(\frac{log_b(M)}{log_b(N)}\) No rules for the quotient of logarithms

Relationship Between Logarithms and Exponentials

These follow from the definition of the 6.1 as the inverse functions for the corresponding exponential functions.

Logarithms and Exponentials:

\(log_b(N)=x \Leftarrow\Rightarrow b^x=N\)
\(log_b(b^x)=x\)
\(b^{log_b(x)}=x\)
