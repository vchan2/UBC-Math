Factoring

When we factor we are applying the distributive law in reverse--reversing what we did when multiplying (which we did in the previous section). Note that we can always check our factoring by multiplying out our factored expression to see that we get what we started with.

Removing a Common Factor:

If we remove a common factor from each of the terms in an expression we are reversing the distributive law:

\(ab+ac=a(b+c)\)

Note the following special cases:

\(-a-b=-(a+b)\)
\((a-b)=-(b-a)\)

Grouping Items

Even though all the terms may not have a common factor, we can sometimes factor by first grouping the terms and then removing a common factor. This method is reversing the distribution law applied to factors with more than one term. To illustrate this concept, let's begin by considering 4 terms. If the expression is of the form \(ac+ad+bc+bd\), then we can group the first two terms and the last two terms:
$$(ac+ad)+(bc+bd) = a(c+d)+b(c+d) = (a+b)(c+d)$$
Notice that another way to group is to pair the first term with the third term:
$$(ac+bc)+(ad+bd) = (a+b)c+(a+b)d = (a+b)(c+d)$$
The trick is to pair the first term with another term that shares a common factor. Finally, be aware that this grouping method will not always work; the terms must be of a special form as above.
